    Learning Goals

    The learning goals are labelled:

    Knowledge & Insight
    • Knowledge and insight with the correct breadth and depth of the IT lifecycle phases: Analysis, Advise, Design, Realisation and Manage & Control.
    • Level of assignment complexity and independence and own contribution the assignment asks for.
    • Additional explanation: An above-average complex assignment, an assignment in a complex setting, or an assignment where the student has shown more than average independence leads to a higher assessment.
    Analysis | Problem definition
    • Clear definition and scoping of the context, problem or challenge.
    • Problem analysis with appropriate breadth and depth; student can argue who the stakeholders are and how they are involved in his project.
    Advise
    • Correct balancing of interests, possibilities, costs and benefits.
    • Format and content appropriate to assignment and context.
    • Additional explanation: The project plan provides advice on project approach, scope and design. Advice can be given throughout the internship or graduation project and be recorded in various professional products. For example in a research report, blog, or as minutes of a sprint review. Regarding portfolio: the reading guide clarifies which professional products have an advice component.
    Design
    • Format and content appropriate to assignment and context.
    • In line with analysis and advice and sufficiently elaborated for realisation.
    • Additional explanation: The most important design decisions are substantiated.
    Realisation
    • Matches the design.
    • Scope and quality appropriate to the problem definition and context.
    • Useful to client.
    • Additional explanation: In all cases, a (partially) functioning IT product was realised within the project. This can be as a prototype (for more complex problems) or a working (partial) product.
    Manage & Control
    • Method and tooling arranged for professional performance, appropriate for the assignment.
    • Worked according to arranged method and tooling.
    • Additional explanation: This concerns both the design and implementation of the IT process from a management perspective (for example with a scrum approach) and more technical processes such as version management.
    Prof. skills

      Judgement

    • Draws up appropriate research and sub-questions.
    • Applies methods within the appropriate research strategies correctly, with taking care for validity and reliability.
    • Reports own work methodically and substantively with the research cycle.
    • Additional explanation: The student shows investigative attitude during the entire project and this is not limited to a certain project phase or aspect. Research questions can be dynamic during the course of the research project. Not every choice requires the same degree of thoroughness of research.

      • Communication

    • Reports orally and in writing about an assignment carried out at a company or institution, expresses in a clear, structured text and sets out his views.
    • Collaborates effectively with professionals in a company or institution.
    • Additional explanation: With thesis or internship report, the thesis or report must be independently understandable and meet the usual document standards (including structure, table of contents, summary, references). For portfolio grading, the reading guide must be independently understandable and meet all document standards. The other professional products must be sufficiently comprehensible for the reader who is familiar with the relevant domain. The student can compensate for a lesser documentation competence through an oral explanation. A minimum level of written communication remains necessary (text must be unambiguous for peers in the relevant domain)

      • Learning ability

    • Describes professional talents and development ambitions in relation to the IT profession.
    • Uses self-reflection and received feedback to improve own functioning in the IT profession.
    • Takes initiatives, takes an independent position, and works independently and result-oriented on the professional assignment.
    • Additional explanation In thesis or internship report, evaluation should reflect on learning experiences during the project. For portfolio grading, reflection and learning capacity must be demonstrated in the reading guide or in a separate product referred to.

    What is the IxD Lectorship?

    The centre of expertise Interaction Design IXD is a research chair at Fontys School of Information and Communication Technology, department of Fontys University of Applied Sciences, Eindhoven, The Netherlands. The Lectorship has a lot of innovative projects running that are being worked on by Fontys ICT lecturers and students and are often sponsored by partners.

    How did I get into contact with the IxD Lectorship?

    In December 2020 I was still on the lookout for an internship. Through my internship coordinator I got wind of the Music Enabled Running (MER) project which was one of the many IxD projects running at the time. I read what the project was about on the website of the Lectorship IxD and decided to contact the project members directly.

    Image: IxD Lectorship

    What is the MER project?

    Initially the aim of the project was to prevent injuries with the help of sonification. This focus eventually shifted towards a new goal. Currently speaking (2021), the new goal of the MER project is to research how sonification, music and sound in general can motivate people to be (and stay) active.

    The assignment

    Read the paragraphs below to read about the assignment.

    New assignment

    Unfortunately the assignment of designing and developing the running app had already been taken. Luckily Olaf and Bernd-Jan came up with a new, challenging assignment for me:

    "How can we, the MER project, professionally present ourselves and our work to stakeholders so that they get informed and can use our research (products)?"

    After having discussed what end products I would deliver, I translated the assignment into the following Design Challenge below 🡫

    Design Challenge

    Design and develop a platform on which project info/data is provided and apply the designed style guide to the platform so that the PR objectives are met and the stakeholders are informed about the project.

    I accepted the proposed assignment and thus after signing some papers I officially became an IxD Lectorship intern.

    My first week

    In my first week of the internship there was a standup meeting planned on Monday and Thursday. The schedule remained like this throughout the internship.

    In this week I setup a few things like writing a project plan, creating a scrum board for planning and reading up on the project so that I knew in what direction the project was headed and what stakeholders were involved. Furthermore, I thought about what kind of platform would be most suitable for the Design Challenge. I decided a website would be the best platform to provide stakeholders with info/data, because:

    • Websites are very accessible; websites can easily be shared through links. A native app for example can't easily be shared.
    • Websites can contain a lot of information; the stakeholders will most likely be active on a desktop screen which means there's a lot of space available to provide information (in the right way). This is useful since a lot of research output will have to be provided. Apps made for mobile devices have a lot less space to work with since mobile devices are significantly smaller.
    • Websites are easy to maintain since there's a lot of support (documentation, tools & communities) available. Native or hybrid apps can be tricky to maintain because of certain exclusive native features and subscriptions.

    Trends

    After deciding the platform was going to be a website I started looking for inspiration from innovative projects/businesses with regard to human vitality that used a website as platform.

    The websites I took inspiration from are:

    • Arion; Arion offers innovative running wearables and software. Their main goal is to sell their products. Arion showcases their products on their home/landing page but they also tell a lot about themselves as a brand.
    • Data Enabled Running; Just like the MER project, Data Enabled Running is an innovative project run by a knowledge institution (TUE) within the field of Human Vitality. Their goal is to spread their research information. What's interesting is that their website is a one pager and they link their research to external websites. This might be because it's easier to spread their research by posting their research onto known research platforms.
    • Alphabeats; Alphabeats offers innovative software. They sell neurofeedback technology that reduces stress. Their main goal is to sell their products. Alphabeats has a lot of 'Call To Action' elements on their website. They are clearly trying to activate the user to engage with their products. On the home/landing page they clearly want to shift the user's attention to their products.
    • DeX; DeX is a platform made by Fontys ICT software students. On this platform students can upload their projects so that other can use these resources and can take inspiration from them. Their goal is dissemination of projects. What's interesting and possibly useful to the MER website is that they have a research card for each project that's posted on the website and that the user can filter them.

    Observations

    I noticed that pretty much all of these websites had a video as hero view background. I also noticed that most of the websites had a lot of different sections that were distinguishable by their difference in background colours.

    Interviewing IxD Lecturers

    In order to find out what clients/partners like the Vitality Living Lab (VLL) expected to see from the MER project I spoke to Ferry Wonders, who was in contact with the VLL and other partners and acted like a product owner of the MER project. About once every month he joined our stand up meeting on Monday or Thursday to get updated on the progress. His function was to ask questions/be critical.

    Q: The Vitality Living Lab is an important stakeholder for the MER Project. Can you tell me what the VLL wants or would like to see? And what about other partners?

    A: "Aside from results they would very much like to see the progression of the project. At the moment the project is only being presented and explained during events like ICT In Practice. If there's a website or some sort of other platform that contains all the information that stakeholders need and is accessible 24/7 then that would be a lot more convenient. This applies to all the public relations we, the IxD Lectorship, have."

    Valuable info from the interview

    The stakeholders want a place where they can look up all the info about the project at anytime. Furthermore, they want to stay updated with the latest progression.

    Interviewing Data Based Minor Students and Lecturers

    In order to find out how the DDBL minor cooperates with the MER project and what the students need to succeed their minor, I spoke to Bart van Gennip (DDBL minor coordinator and in contact with VLL).

    Q: What kind of resources do the students need in order to succeed their assignment? What kind of learning goals and/or requirements do the students need to meet?

    A: "The client/partner always directly delivers the needed resources to the students. SQL databases, JSON files, CSV and Excel sheets are most common."

    Valuable info from the interview

    A dataset with data in JSON or CSV format is the best way to deliver the students a dataset that will be most useful to them.

    In order to find out how the AI minor cooperates with the MER project and what the students need to succeed their minor, I spoke to Martijn Lamers (AI minor coordinator).

    Q: What kind of resources do the students need? Are there any tools that need to be delivered? What kind of requirements do the students need to meet in order to succeed their minor?

    A: "A big dataset (includes at least 10.000 records) has to be delivered within the first three weeks. The students will then be able to start working on the project and try to deliver products like an analysis report, a dashboard and an algorithm. They often use Python with the Anaconda IDE and they usually visualise the data with Jupiter Notebooks, Power BI or Tableau. It's best if the data is delivered in a raw format so that the students have more of a challenge when trying to clean up the data and use it."

    Valuable info from the interview

    The dataset will need to contain a minimum of 10.000 records and needs to be in a raw format so that students will be able to meet their learning goals requirements more easily. Excel sheets, CSV files or databases are allowed. The dataset has to be delivered to the students as soon as possible.

    In order to find out what kind of resources the minor students that cooperate with the MER project need, I spoke to three international DDBL minor students. A few days before I interviewed them they received a research paper and a dataset from the MER project. I had to find out if those resources were sufficient for the students.

    Q: Did you feel like something was missing? Information about the data for example?

    A: "My difficulty is that I don’t understand much about the technique or the terms so I have to search everything online so that I understand what the data is talking about. My suggestion is that if there’s any data visualizations for the students who is new with this project to have the overview of the data that would be better than just some datasets. It’s not messy but I can’t get a good overview after seeing the data."

    Valuable info from the interview

    The students need an explanation of the technical terms that are used in the research paper so that they can fully understand how the dataset works.

    First Design Iterations

    Below are the first sets of designs that were partially based on the information gathered from the interviews. These are the first few designs I made before I was able to interview the stakeholders. The first couple of designs were purely created to get an idea of what information was going to be needed and what the hierarchy/layout of the website was going to look like. This also helped me explain what I had in mind to the project members and they could then give a lot of feedback and new ideas/requirements. We discussed what was needed on the website in every standup meeting during this period.

    Home/landing page - V1

    This is the first iteration of the landing/home page.

    At the top we have a pretty standard navigation bar. I added two translation buttons. One button for the international stakeholders and one for the Dutch stakeholders. This feature wasn't included in the final version of the design since I decided it was more time efficient to put everything on the site in English. The Dutch can understand English perfectly well so that wouldn't be a problem.

    The first section of the page, the so-called 'Hero View', needed to summarize and clarify what we, as a project group, were researching. I came up with a visualization that would explain this in six steps. When I tried to explain it I noticed I couldn't come up with six steps that would explain everything. The other project members found this difficult as well. Because of these difficulties we decided it was best if I designed something different for the hero view.

    In this design the 'About' section contains a textual explanation of who the project members are and what the goal of the MER project is. Initially I placed the 'About' section this high up on the 'Home' page since it was rather important to explain what the MER Project was about.

    Beneath the 'About' section I placed the logo's of the partners that support the project. Early on it was known that we'd have to give credits to the partners. The 'News' section I placed below the partners. In this section partners can quickly see what's new and when we will be present on events such as ICT in Practice.

    Home/landing page - V2

    In this version I added a 'Timeline' section, a 'Newsletter' section and a footer.

    I added the 'Timeline' section, because new or potential partners need to catch up with what happened in the project.

    I added the 'Newsletter' section since some stakeholders want consistent updates. Subscribing to a newsletter that is sent around every 3 months will provide that.

    I added the footer, because if users scrolled all the way down and haven't found what they're looking for they might be able to find it in the footer.

    "A last resort for hard-to-find content. Sometimes users turn to the footer for additional options that might not be present in the global navigation." ~ Nielsen Norman Group

    The 'Contact' section and websites of the IxD Lectorship and VLL aren't present in the navigation bar, but they are present in the footer.

    Home/landing page - V3/V4

    After having difficulties with the first hero view design (see V1) I designed a new one. In this version I made sure so-called Call to Action elements were present. Call to Action elements are needed to direct the different types of stakeholders to the sections they are likely most interested in. For example, the researchers are more interested in the research output and the partners/PR related parties are more interested in recent news/discoveries.

    On the left side of the hero view the idea is to have a textual animation that shortly explains what we do as a project group. This information is relevant to all the stakeholders.

    I implemented a tab system in the 'About' section since a lot more information had to be added after one of the brainstorm sessions with the project members.

    In the hero view below I changed the design slightly. In the last design two animations were displayed, but I decided this would not work well with the background video. While the animation summarizes what we do, the purpose of the background video is to go a bit more in-depth/into detail what this (sonification, movement, AI, music, etc) exactly means.

    Output page - V1

    The output page is the place where all the research and products of the MER project are viewable. This mainly includes research papers, research tests and findings.

    On the left side I put a filter system so that the stakeholders would be able to find the output they're most interested in.

    In the center of the page I placed a grid of cards. Each card summarizes what the output is about by the displayed title, paragraph and labels/tags. Below the labels/tags that indicate the subject of the output the user can see around what date the output was worked on and how many people worked on it.

    When the user clicks on a card a new window pops up. The output is displayed here. The output can be displayed in a lot of different ways; a collection of images and text, a PDF, iframes, videos etc. At the top of the window the user can see general information; the data on which the output was produced, who worked on the output and potentially an external link to direct stakeholders to, for example, a Google Drive directory with data or source code in it which the stakeholders can use.

    Output page - V2

    The project members gave feedback on V1 of the Output page. Their feedback was that the filter panel wasn't really going to be necessary since they didn't have a lot of output anyway and that only the most interesting output was going to be displayed. The project members also told me it would be ideal if the different types of stakeholder groups would each be able to find what they want to see with one click of their mouse and not with a lot of filter options.

    The idea of the first design is to redirect the different groups of stakeholders to the output they want to see. The first card describes the partners/PR related parties. The second card describes the external researchers.

    Clicking on the first card redirects the users to the page below. That version of the output page is meant to only show the most interesting things and summarize them (no long research papers). If the user clicks on the second card they'll see the output page with the filter panel (see V1).

    Hero View Video & Animations

    When I looked at the trends of websites with a similar subject (human vitality) I noticed that the majority had a hero view with a video as background and/or animations. I discussed this with the project members and they agreed that it would be good to have some sort of video and/or animation that would visually explain the project. I decided to do some workshop prototyping by making a video prototype first since I would be able to show the project members what I had in mind in an easier way. Down below you can view the iterations.

    Since a lot is being researched within the MER Project is decided it would be necessary to sum up some of the keywords like 'sonification'. The idea was to put these keywords in a looping text animation. I made these animations in After Effects to show the project members what I had in mind.

    I played a bit with the easing effects, but ultimately I decided it would be best to use a simple linear animation since it would draw too much attention away from the background video.

    After a few more iterations this was the outcome. The idea of this was to hold the background video on pause and first let the animation play. After the animation was finished the video would play and become the center of attention. This ensures the hero view doesn't show an overload of information to the user.

    Brainstorm sessions

    After the feedback on version 2 of the home/landings page we decided it was best to have a big brainstorm session with the whole project group in which we tried to come up with as many categories and items that were needed on the website in order to explain the project.

    This method is called the Lotus Blossom method. The yellow card represents the central theme. Eight conceptual themes (the purple cards) flow out from the central theme and each of them are used as central team to generate 8 more themes (the text around the purple cards).

    Developing the website

    Read the paragraphs below to see what info I gathered from the interviews and how I translated this into designs.

    Preparation

    Before I started developing, the project members asked me if I was planning to use any frameworks/libraries or other tools & methods. At first I was planning to simply use plain/vanilla HTML/CSS/JavaScript and maybe add some libraries for small tasks like an email system and lightboxes along the way since I was making a simple informative website with minimal input from the users. I set up a GitHub repo in which me and the project members would be able to add code. When I had set up the foundation of the website, I asked the project members to help fill in the content (e.g. research papers). To ensure we wouldn't get any complicated merge conflicts we worked together by using different branches. Each commit got a small description of what changed.

    Using big frameworks like React or Vue seemed like it would be overkill for such a simple website. Frameworks can be bulky which drags the website's performance down and can overcomplicate things which can endanger maintainability. On the other hand frameworks overall reduce development time if they don't have too much of a learning curve. I decided to search for a small framework that could help me write the code quicker. This brought my attention to TailwindCSS.

    TailwindCSS

    TailwindCSS (like the name suggests) is a CSS framework. Tailwind has a lot of so-called 'utility classes' available to use that are basically shortened CSS properties that you can directly insert as a class in your HTML elements. It's easy to learn so for the project members it shouldn't be a problem to adapt to if they would have to make changes especially since you don't necessarily need to use Tailwind's utilities. They can also use vanilla CSS and overwrite Tailwind if necessary if they prefer that.

    An example of what TailwindCSS looks like.

    As you can see in the example above, a disadvantage of Tailwind is that it's more difficult to read the CSS properties compared to a standard CSS file. On the other hand, using these abbreviated classes in only one file (as opposed to a separated CSS and HTML file with longer property names) helps shorten development time a lot. In addition, it's quicker to see what styles are applied to what elements.

    Requirements & Tools

    While I was developing the website, the project members and myself came up with a few more suggestions (see the list below) of requirements to meet and tools to use.

    • Try to make the website more maintainable/CMS like; after I developed most of the website, the project members began to help fill in the content of the website by adding research in the form of text and grid items. They noticed that they had to copy and edit a lot of HTML each time they added new items. Some sort of component system that simplifies this process was needed to keep the website maintainable. One of the project members, Olaf Janssen, made a POC with Metalsmith (a markdown to HTML generator) to see if the process could be simplified. This worked, but still had a few minor problems. Currently I'm trying to see what I can do to improve this system.
    • Use an email system that makes sure the input fields don't get spammed; I used FormSubmit for this. FormSubmit receives the input that the user sends and sends these to your email address (a kind of middleman). They give the option to turn on reCAPTCHA so that made it possible for me to meet the requirement
    • Make the website usable for all devices; this was discussed just before I started developing. I only made designs for desktop devices, because I assumed the stakeholders would visit the website while they were at work behind their desktops based on the information I gathered. Since I didn't explicitly ask/research what devices the stakeholders used I relied on the information Olaf gave me. Olaf told me that during events like ICT in Practice it would be handy to quickly whip out his phone or an ipad and show stakeholders/partners the website when talking to them. I ended up only making one extra design for mobile users, but only for the home page since this needed more visual changes compared to the other pages and I was running short on time. Luckily, TailwindCSS made it very easy and fast to make the website responsive.
    • Make the website shareable for social media (posts); The project members were thinking about posting some of their research on platforms like LinkedIn. They suggested looking into the Open Graph protocol. Unfortunately, implementing this in the MER website wasn't ideal since I already defined the structure of the website which didn't work well with the OG protocol.

      Unfortunately, using the OG protocol wouldn't work well with the way the MER website is structured.       OG Workaround method

      I read this Medium article about how you can create a dynamic Open Graph image or GIF for your website. The article says that you can use a tool called 'Pickapic' that allows you to change an OG image/GIF conditionally. For example, you can choose to display one image each Wednesday and a different image on Thursday.
